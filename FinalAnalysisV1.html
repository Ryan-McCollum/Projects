Introduction

A designed experiment conducted by Miami University Microbiologist Marcia Lee looks to investigate the effects of different aloe based treatments on the prevention of fungal morphogenesis. This designed experiment was run on the fungus Candida Albicans, which is a part of normal microbial flora in mammals. However, when this fungus grows hyphae, it can attach in different ways and form and release buds that travel through the body that can start infection and disease. However, there is a possibility that using aloe, or aloe based compounds may be able to stop the formation of hypha and therefore reduce the potential for infection and disease from Candida Albicans. In this experiment, 7 different aloe based treatments are tested as well as 1 control group that receives no aloe based treatment. Each treatment was replicated 5 times, and in each replicate 200 cells were counted to see how many did not form anything, how many formed Pseudohyphae, Hyphae and Buds. In this experiment, Pseudohyphae are labeled as PH, Hyphae, or ‘Germ Tubes’ are labeled GT, and Buds are labeled Buds.

Methods

The main hypotheses in question are as follows: Hypothesis 1: Exposure of Candida albicans cells to each of the seven treatments results in statistically significant reductions in formation of hyphae compared to non-exposed cells Hypothesis 2: Exposure of Candida albicans cells to A and A-SA treatments results in greater reductions in formation of germ tubes compared to treatments B and B-SA. Hypothesis 3: Exposure of Candida albicans cells to treatment Whole A results in the greatest reduction in germ tubes as compared to the other 6 treatments.

The hypotheses can each be tested through a series of contrasts that varies depending on the hypothesis and what result is of interest testing. However, 1 model based on the distribution of the proportions will be built that will be used in all contrast comparisons for each hypothesis. The contrasts for hypothesis 1, which compares each treatment to the control group, will be set up with null and alternative hypotheses as in equations (1) and (2).

(1) Null: \(p_{control} = p_{i}, i=1,...,7\) Where each \(p_{i}\) represents a different treatment proportion and \(p_{control}\) is the proportion of total cells that had some occurrence of hypha formation out of 200.

(2) Alternative: \(p_{control} > p_{i}, i=1,...,7\)

The Contrast for hypothesis 2 compares treatments with compound A to those with compound B. Since there are 2 of each, there are 4 different comparisons for hypothesis 2 (A to B, A to B-SA, A-SA to B, and A-SA to B-SA). These contrasts have null and alternative hypotheses in equations (3) and (4).

(3) Null: \(p_{compA} = p_{compB}; p_{compA} = p_{compB-SA};\) \(p_{compA-SA} = p_{compB}; p_{compA-SA} = p_{compB-SA}\)

(4) Alternative: \(p_{compA} < p_{compB}; p_{compA} < p_{compB-SA};\) \(p_{compA-SA} < p_{compB}; p_{compA-SA} < p_{compB-SA}\)

Lastly, the contrast for hypothesis 3 investigates if the Whole A aloe treatment results in a greater reduction in hypa formation than any other treatment. Thus it has similar contrasts to hypothesis 1, with the Whole A compound acting as the control being compared against. The null and alternative hypothesis are in equations (5) and (6).

(5) Null: \(p_{WholeA} = p_{i}, i=1,...,6\) Where each \(p_{i}\) represents a different treatment proportion for each of the 6 treatments that are not the control group or Whole A group, and \(p_{WholeA}\) is the proportion of total cells that had some occurrence of hypha formation out of 200.

(6) Alternative: \(p_{WholeA} < p_{i}, i=1,...,6\)

Model Building

When building a model, it must appropriately fit the data being modeled. Since the data is proportional, [0,1], a distribution needs to fit this interval. Initially it would seem that the data is continuous, however, each possible proportion is a frequency divided by 200, so the data is technically discrete. A beta distribution therefore is not ideal, as a beta distribution cannot have values of 0 and 1, but in the hypha growth proportion is is possible to obtain 0 hypha growing cells out of 200 or 200 out of 200 cells can have hypha. Therefore, some sort of counting distribution is the most appropriate. When choosing between a binomial or poisson distribution, there is not necessarily a better answer, but due to potential for better interpretability of results, this report will focus on the binomial-logistic model case. In the case of hypha growth, there appears to be zero-inflation. This means that when selecting the best model, a binomial-logistic model with a logit link function can be fit first, but it needs to be tested for over dispersion. This can be tested by looking at the sum or squared residual deviance of the binomial-logistic model of log odds divided by the residual degrees of freedom. This results in a Chi-squared test statistic that if close to 1, would suggest no overdispersion. If the data turns out to be over disperesed, then a quasi-binomial-logistic regression would be more appropriate and satisfy more model assumptions. A binomial-logistic model would have model form in equation (7).

\[(7): ln(\frac{P(\hat{p}=1)}{P(\hat{p}=0)}) = \beta_0+\beta_{control}+\beta_{WholeA}+\beta_{A}+\beta_{A-SA}+\beta_{B}+\beta_{B-SA}+\beta_{A,B}+\beta_{A-SA,B-SA}\] Where each beta is an indicator depending on which treatment is being represented. Therefore the log odds of hypha growth is predicted by the overall model intercept and a beta parameter depending on treatment. After fitting this model, it can be tested for over dispersion with \(\phi = \frac{Deviance_{residual}}{DegreesFreedom_{residual}} > 1\) indicating over dispersion. If this is the case, the quasi-binomial logistic regression model will build a model similar to equation (7), but with a dispersion parameter left to be estimated by the model. One thing to consider is that this models the log odds of the total hypha growth being 0 or 1, and normally a set cutoff point would be set with this type of modeling, say 50%, and everything above .5 would be rounded up to 1, and below rounded to 0 to predict a binary outcome of hypha growth. However, since this study is not focused on a binary outcome, the predictions from this model and coefficients can be interpreted without establishing a cutoff point, since there is no reason to round up or down to 1 and 0 for binary outcomes. One last important detail in using a binomial model is that it assumes the response variable is also binary. Therefore, the errors produced will be incorrect. To adjust for this, each proportion can be weighted by the number of trails done for that observation. Since each observation in this study is done by counting 200 cells, the number of trials for each observation is weighted the same at 200. One benefit of this modeling is that there are not a lot of assumptions, mainly just independence and linearity. Independence of observations is confirmed through the design of the experiment, and linearity of the log odds of total hypha formation to treatment type is checked by a Box-Tidwell test.

The results of each contrast comparison will be output in an odds ratio format. For example, the odds ration of the first contrast, Control vs Whole A, can be interpreted as: \[\frac{\frac{P(HyphaInControl)}{P(NoHyphaInControl)}}{\frac{P(HyphaInWholeA)}{P(NoHyphaInWholeA)}}\] This is a complicated formula, and the best way to interpret it is that if the odds ratio of a contrast is much larger than 1, then the odds that Hypha is found in the Control group in this situation is much larger than the odds that Hypha is found in the Whole A group, and vice-versa for a odds ratio lower than 1 (but above 0). The definition of odds is defined as the probability that an event will occur divided by the probability that it wont. So an odds ratio greater than 1 means that an event is more likely to occur than not, odds ratio less than 1 means an event is less likely to occur than not, and an odds ratio equal to 1 means an event is equally likely to occur than not to occur. In the context of this experiment, the ‘event’ of interest is a singular cell having some sort of hypha formation.

Conclusion

based on the results of the contrasts, it appears that any of the treatments tested result in a significant reduction in odds of hypha formation compared to the control group. Therefore, compared to applying nothing, any of the aloe based treatments would be beneficial in preventing hypha formation. Further, the second hypothesis confirmed that the A based compounds (A and A-SA) resulted in significant reductions in odds of hypha formation as compared to the synthetic B compound. However, the test between synthetic A and compound B was close to the significance level, meaning further testing is probably a good idea, and there was no significant difference in odds between compound A and B. Overall, the best conclusion from hypothesis 2 is that the synthetic compound B treatment is worse than either compound A option. Lastly, hypothesis 3 showed that the Whole A aloe treatment was significantly the best treatment, having up to \(\frac{1}{6}^{th}\) the odds of hypha formation as other treatments.
